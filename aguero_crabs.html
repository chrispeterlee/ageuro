Peak Year
2017 was the golden age — 28,000 messages, nearly triple the 2016 founding. February alone saw 4,140 messages in a single month. The group had found its voice and absolutely would not stop using it.
The Long Slide
From the 2017 peak, activity roughly halved by 2022. Life happened. People moved abroad, got jobs, had kids. The chat didn't die — it just aged into something quieter and (marginally) more considered.
Lockdown Spike
2020 bucked the trend slightly. Turns out when nobody can go anywhere, the WhatsApp group becomes a lifeline. October 31, 2020 — 619 messages watching rugby at midnight — is the single busiest day in the chat's history.
The Comeback
2024 and 2025 show a modest revival. Up from the 2022–23 trough. Perhaps friendship is, in fact, a renewable resource. Or there was just more rugby on.

In Memoriam

Every group has them. The ones who were there, then weren't. This is not a condemnation — life happens, group chats drift, phones get lost. But the data is the data.

A founding member. 178 messages in 2016. Held on through 2019 (29 messages), 2020 (42), 2021 (15). Then five in 2022 and gone. His most used word in later years was "lads" — always reaching out, less and less often.
The Partial Fades
Jamie Martin Grace — the group's founder — has been trending down since his 2017 peak of 2,488 messages. In 2025 he sent just 243. He's still there, still sharp when he appears, but increasingly rare.

Chris Scollard — who changed the group icon in the first five minutes — went from 3,243 in 2017 to 299 in 2025, including one 38-day gap of silence. The co-founder, fading quietly.
